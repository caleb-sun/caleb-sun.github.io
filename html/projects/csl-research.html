    UX Research

    Evaluating a critical user flow for fleet-wide release.

    Skills

    UX Research
    UX Design
    Prototyping
    UI Design

    Duration

    Two weeks

    Tools

    Figma

    CSL was preparing to introduce appointments to their long-established walk-in plasma donation process. This project was conducted to streamline that transition.

    Problem Space

    A new type of donation

    CSL has always conducted plasma donations on a walk-in basis. However, there was a push from business stakeholders to introduce an appointment scheduling capability to their locations. As the only touchpoint between CSL and plasma donors outside physical centers, the Plasma app was leveraged as the medium to introduce this functionality.

    Having already designed baseline screens for the new flow, there was a push to test and improve before production caught up, so that the transition from walk-in to appointment would be as smooth as possible.

    Project Overview

    Created technology usage guide for 10 participants.

    Crafted fully functioning prototype for testing usage.

    Assisted in research synthesis and modified designs in response to feedback.

    Results

    10 participants, 2 rounds, massively improved designs

    Through two rounds of testing, we were able to identify areas to improve the existing designs, areas to put back to the drawing board, and areas to scrap completely. We also established protocol for future trials and, more importantly, validated the importance of user input during the design process.

    Driving Questions

    Is the existing user journey seamless and easy to understand?

    Another designer had collaborated closely with business stakeholders to create a user-appointment journey, but given the differences between CSL and other healthcare apps, we needed to evaluate whether the deviations in the app from traditional user mental models would impede its usage.

    How can we evaluate participant mental models around plasma donation?

    A goal of the entire CSL Plasma app is to educate and inform new donors about what to expect when donating plasma. When creating this flow, we needed to ensure that users could learn and be prepared for their appointment, and a challenge for the project was to come up with an effective way to evaluate users for their understanding through both questions and generative activities.

    How can we facilitate usability testing in a remote environment?

    Considering that this was the first usability testing trials conducted by CSL in-house ever, a challenge for this project was to establish best practices and procedure with no previously-established protocol. This included preparing technology set-up guides, detailed documentation, and robust tester-friendly prototypes.

    Technology Onboarding

    Creating documentation and set-up guides of testing tools for participants.

    I created a visual guide for technology set-up for participants to reference as they prepared for their testing session, keeping in mind that participants may have varying levels of familiarity with technology like Microsoft Teams and Figma.

    Working Prototype

    Robust prototyping for testing.

    I created a fully-functional Figma prototype to allow for realistic testing, taking advantage of local variables to simulate live data. The decision to use a Figma prototype as opposed to a production prototype was based on the difficulty of facilitating screen-sharing remotely. To empower economically diverse participants, we prioritized universiality in the testing methodologies.

    Robust testing prototype

    A prototype that can be messed with, as live as a production app without the hassle of screen-sharing.

    Navigate between flows

    Quick navigation between different flows utilized at different points in the testing process.

    Editable data

    Live data using Figma's variable and conditional prototyping to increase realism and personalization.

    User feedback

    UI confusion

    Participants identified confusion regarding certain UI elements like the calendar, in which the colors chosen did not distinguish well between active and inactive states.

    Mental model of appointments

    Participants did not understand the difference between two types of center visits presented in the app: appointments and express walk-ins.

    Missing information

    Participants did not feel prepared for their appointment after booking, and the notification flow in the latter half of the testing run did not improve their understanding.

    UI Changes

    Adjusted UI elements to clarify element states and interactive-ity.
